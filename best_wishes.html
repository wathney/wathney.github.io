Best Wishes


By Lars I. Wathney

www.Wathney.com



I can hear him outside, my dead brother. Circling the house, testing the doors and windows, searching for a way in. Sometimes I hear him scrabbling at the walls and moaning my name in a hollow voice I don't recognize.


He attempts to persuade me, “Come open the door, Tommy, its your brother Dave! There's nothing to be afraid of, I'm not gonna hurt you! Come and join me down the well, there's room for one more!”


I have tried wearing earplugs, but not being able to hear him is worse. I wouldn't be able to hear if he found a way in. Sometimes he is silent for hours, and I lay in bed, or hide in a corner, listening for him. As I am sure he stands outside, motionless, with his head cocked, listening for me. I dare not look out the window, for I know that if I do, I'll see his dead eyes staring back at me. Eyes glowing with a ghostly shine, as though reflecting the blue-green light from some unseen alien moon. Last night I briefly nodded off, and dreamt that the forest surrounding the house was filled with glowing eyes. I awoke with a start, and covered in a clammy sweat that reeked of fear. The bed beneath me, along with the clothes I wore, were damp, and brought back bad memories of wetting the bed. I was a worrisome child, afflicted with anxieties and nightmares at a young age. Many were forgotten upon waking, but one in particular was still vivid in my mind. I was being chased by a pack of slavering wolves, loping behind me, always gaining, and nipping at my heels as I try to run. I lean forward at an impossible angle, trying to pump my legs faster, but the effort is futile. I move in slow-motion, like running underwater. I wake up just before they catch me.


Some nights I would awake from a dream, with memory of the particulars already fading, leaving only a vague sense of foreboding. Upon awakening, I would notice a dark figure in my room. Just standing there at the foot of the bed, or in the corner of the room, on the edge of my peripheral vision. I would be paralyzed, unable to move, or scream, or flee, and unable to remove the suffocating weight pressing down on my chest. That feeling of being half-awake, like a state of drunkenness. Things don't really make sense, and your thoughts don't connect together the right way. After some time the figure would fade, and movement would return to my body. The dreamy drunken feeling would slowly fade as well, leaving only the foreboding, and a hazy memory. Sometimes, instead of a shadowy figure, I would see giant hairy spiders crawling on the walls, or a creature sitting on my chest.


My nightmares stopped the summer of my sixth year on this planet. It began when my family took a trip to visit my father's father, Grandpa Whelan, who lived on a farm in Elk City, Idaho. Elk City was a small town, with a population, of roughly 200 people. Technically it was not even a town, rather an unincorporated community. It sat in the middle of Nez Perce National Forest, and was surrounded by the largest expanse of wilderness in the lower 48. The only way in or out of Elk City was on Idaho State Highway 14. It started just outside Grangeville, and wound it's way through the forest, following the South Fork Clearwater River upstream for 50 miles, then dead-ending in Elk City. Dave and I grew up in Portland, Oregon, and had never visited the farm before. We had, however, met Grandpa Whelan on several occasions when he had visited us in Portland, and liked him very much. He was a cheerful old man, always finding time to entertain us kids. Always ready with a story, many of which I'm sure were subject to slight embellishment. He could often be found in an easy chair, watching TV, or reading a book, while he sipped his beer, and took the occasional puff of sweet-smelling tobacco from his pipe. Dave and I were excited about visiting the farm for the first time, we had heard many of Grandpa Whelan's stories about it. We had also heard the many misadventures Dad had growing up there.


Mom wanted to fly, but Dad wouldn't hear of it. He was not afraid to fly, but thought it a waste of money to fly to a place close enough to drive. If we traveled via plane, the flight itself would only take a few hours, but once you factor in the layover, and the drive into elk city, it looked more like seven or eight hours. By car, it would take about nine hours. I don't think it was the extra hour that Mom was worried about, but being trapped in the car with the rest of us for nine hours. Getting up early to start the drive was not fun. We were supposed to leave the house by 6:00 AM, so I had gotten up at 5:00. Everyone was still tired, and Mom yelling at us to get our rears in gear did not help any. We had no particular appointment to make, but apparently leaving so early ensured we would miss the rush hour traffic across great swaths of undeveloped wilderness.


I did, however, enjoy the ride there. Crossing northern Oregon, cutting through the south-eastern tip of Washington, and then into Idaho, the scenery was beautiful. I spent most of the trip looking out the window from the back seat, watching the trees fly, and occasionally spotting a deer bounding through the underbrush. In Montana I spotted a dead coyote on the side of the road, hit by a car traveling at high-speed. It was almost torn in half, with its back section crushed and mangled. Its eyes bugged out of the skull, looking dull and filmy, and its tongue lolled out the side of its mouth. The stomach had popped open when it had been struck, and intestines lay spilled on the pavement. There was not as much blood as I would have expected. In the movies, when someone is killed, there's blood everywhere. In comparison, this almost looked fake.


We stopped every couple of hours, for a bathroom break, or to get some food. After finishing lunch in Grangeville, we set out towards highway 14, and the 50 mile winding climb up to Elk city. There were fewer signs of civilization, once we left Grangeville, but we did see the occasional fisherman casting his line into the river that paralleled the highway. There were turnouts along the way, where fishermen parked their cars, or tourists stopped to take pictures. It was at one of these where we pulled off the highway, and parked next to a truck with an assortment of fishing gear in the bed. While dad was busy snapping photos, and mom worked the circulation back into her legs, Dave and I climbed down the short embankment to the river. This was a prime opportunity for skipping rocks. We combed the bank, searching for flat, rounded stones to spin out across the water. Dave pitched one out and it hopped a good 8 times before sinking under the surface.


“Not enough Spin,” Dave mused in a sage tone.


Dave was an expert rock skipper, his record was 17 skips; I had seen it with my own eyes. Upstream, I spotted the fisherman to whom the truck belonged, kneeling by the edge of the water. I picked my way closer, to investigate, and saw he was cleaning a fish. The fish lay on its side, on top of a rock. It was flapping its tail back and forth, and working its jaws, gasping for a gillful of water. The fisherman gripped the fish with his left hand, the thumb hooked under the gill to maintain a firm hold on the slimy body. With his right hand he flipped the blade out of a pocket knife, and pressed the tip into the top of the fish's skull. As the blade slid inside, the fish stopped flapping and lay still. He then slit open the belly, and pulled out the guts. He gripped the lump of organs with his left hand, holding the length of intestine still connected to the back of the head taut. Using the knife he sliced it off, and through the lumpy mass back into the water. I watched the ropey length of entrails twisting through the water, and they reminded me of the dead coyote. The fisherman was packing his catch into an ice chest, when he looked up and spotted me watching him.


“If you clean 'em right after you catch 'em, they taste that much better when you cook 'em,” he said, with a wink and a knowing smile.


Mom hollered our names, so Dave and I climbed back up to the car. As we pulled back onto the road, I looked back, and saw the fisherman casting his line back into the water. While we were traveling up hill, the highway lay in a valley, carved by the river it followed. Tree-covered ridges rose from either side, with rocky outcroppings overhanging the road at some points. Clouds of fog drifted lazy down the valley, and as we drove we would hit alternating pockets of obscuring white, and clear air. This lent the last leg of the drive a dreamy atmosphere, and it almost felt like drifting in and out of other worlds. We emerged from the fog into Elk City, and it was smaller than I expected. It was not even really a town, but a few buildings comprising what I suppose one would refer to as “downtown”. There were a couple of stores, a saloon, and a motel, surrounded by a sprawling network of houses and farms. We drove through town (I almost missed it when I blinked), and out to Grandpa's Farm. It was a few miles outside town, and by the time we got there, the handful of businesses were no longer visible. In fact not much of anything was visible, except for the trees surrounding the farm.


Grandpa lived in a large two-story farm house, so Dave and I each got our own room. I chose one on the second story with a window looking out the side of the house. That night, sleeping alone in my room, I dreamt of looking out the window and seeing a large grey wolf outside. He sat unmoving, staring back at me with eyes that glowed. It must have been a dream, for the next day I told Grandpa about it, and he said that there hadn't been any wolves living around those parts for a long time. Apparently they had all been killed by hunters, and farmers.


The next day, I was outside playing near the barn. I sat on the ground digging in the dirt, when Grandpa walked up and stood over me, blotting out the sun.


“You shouldn't be playing out here around the barn, Tommy. There's lots of sharp tools in there that could hurt you,” he scowled down at me with a stern look on his face that I had never seen before.


Then he cracked a smile, saying, “I'll tell you what, come with me, and I will show you something that I think you might like”.


We walked around the other side of the house, the side which my bedroom window faced, and he led me out to a circle of stones towards the edge of the woods.


“Do you want to hear a secret, Tommy, my boy?” He asked me in a conspiratorial whisper, while cupping his hand around the side of his mouth.


I nodded, looking up at him with wide eyes, “You bet I do, I promise not to tell”.


“It was MY grandfather who bought this land back in the 1800's,” Grandpa told me, as he pushed aside the circular plank of wood covering the stones, “And he dug this well himself. As the story goes, he hired himself a traveling magician who specialized in the arcane and mystic arts, to scry him out a good spot to dig. Well this Magician fellow, he pulled out these two bent rods of metal, what you call divining rods. Well he held these rods in his hands, and judging from where they pointed as he walked around, he was able to find the best spot to dig for water. There's still plenty of water down there, but we don't use it anymore. My father had a new well dug closer to the house, so that it could be piped directly inside. We don't use this one for water anymore, but it IS still good for one thing … making wishes”.


Grandpa winked at me and said, “when I was just a boy myself, oh, around your age I suppose … well one day my dog ran away. I cried something awful, so my dad takes me out to this very well, and tells me what I've just told you, about it granting wishes. He points out how the grass circling the well seems to grow more vigorous. He tells me that maybe if I wish for my dog to come back it will. He hands me a coin, so I throw it in and wish for Wolfie to come home, and I be darned if that dog didn't show up at the front door the very next day.”


Grandpa reached into his pocket, and pulled out a penny, “So I think theres something that you might want to be wishing for”.


I leaned up against the edge of the well, and flipped the penny inside, watching as it twirled end over end, before being swallowed by the darkness. A second later I heard a faint splash as it hit the bottom, and I silently wished for my bad dreams to go away.


“Did you think of a good one?” Grandpa asks me.


“You bet I did, and I hope it comes true”.


After that day, my nightmares grew fewer and fewer, until they vanished completely. The next time we visited Grandpa Whelan's farm, was Christmas of the following year. It having been such a long time for a child of my age, the memory of the well and the wish I had made had faded into the dusty recesses of my memory. I never just woke up one day, realizing I didn't have nightmares anymore, it happened sort of gradually, and by the time they were gone, I had no reason to remember. Visiting the farm again reminded me. The more I pondered, the more I realized how amazing it was that the well had granted my wish. While Dave and I were playing outside the day before Christmas eve, I recounted the story Grandpa had told me. I told him about my wish, and about how my nightmares had gone away.


“That doesn't mean the well made them go away, you dope, you could have stopped having them on your own,” Dave said.


“Yeah, but I know it was the well that made them go away,” I told him.


Without really thinking about it we drifted towards the well, and found ourselves standing at the edge of the stone wall surrounding it. Dave pushed aside the wooden cover, and peered inside.


“I don't see what's so special about it, looks like a regular old well to me,” he said.


I dug my hands into my pockets, and found I had a few pennies in one of them.


“I'm going to wish for the present I want,” I told Dave, as I showed him the pennies resting in my palm.


“HA! That'll never work,” Dave scoffed at me. Then, glancing down at the pennies I held, snatched one out of my hand, “But I'll try too, just in case”.


We flipped our pennies into the well, watched them disappear, and listened for the splash. I hear them hit the water, one right after the other, and I wish for a Nintendo.


“What did you wish for, Dave?” I asked my brother.


“Well if I told u that, Tommy boy, it wouldn't come true,” he said with a smile, and slapped me on the back, “Now let's get back to the house. I think dinners almost ready, and I'm starving”.


On Christmas day, I tore into my big present under the tree, and found a Nintendo. I stared slack-jawed and too excited to speak. I managed to mumble something incoherent as I opened the box. Then Mom and Dad wheeled a bicycle into the room, and it was Dave's turn to stare dumb-founded. It was the 21 speed mountain bike he'd been wanting since last summer. After a few solid hours of breaking in my new Nintendo, I joined Dave outside, where he was riding his new bike.


Dave does a two-wheel skid up next to me, and says, “You know this bike is what I wished for at that old well, the other day.”


“I wished for a Nintendo, too. I told you that well could grant wishes”, I whisper to him.


“That doesn't prove that the well granted our wishes, we could have been getting these presents already, and wishing for them wouldn't do anything”.


“I don't know, I still think it was the well,” I tell him, with a bent head, and a defeated sound in my voice.


Dave popped a wheelie and rode off on his new bike. I went back upstairs to try and rescue the princess. That night, I stayed up late playing Super Mario Bros. When I finally turned it off, the house was quiet. It sounded as though everyone else has gone to sleep. As I climbed into bed, my eye was caught by a flickering in the corner of my vision. When I went to look out the window, it looked like a faint greenish glow was seeping out from underneath the cover of the well.


After New Years, we went back home to Portland and again, memory of the well faded. When I started high school, Dave was a Senior and playing football for the Varsity team. He was pretty good, and had dreams of a full-ride scholarship. He even had a beautiful girlfriend, Ashley. She was a great person too, despite the fact that she was a cheerleader. She was always nice to me. When she came over, she would talk to me like she actually cared about what I was saying, and not just making polite conversation. Sometimes she would even play video games with me. Dave had it all going on. Then the femur above his left knee began to ache. Reasoning it was only growing pains, he thought nothing of it. But the pain didn't go away. After a couple of weeks, its got worse. I noticed he was starting to limp slightly. His bedroom shared a wall with mine, and I could hear him at night, sobbing in pain. Dave finally visited the doctor, who told him that he had Osteosarcoma, a type of bone cancer. He underwent a procedure which the doctor called limb salvage surgery. They removed a section of his femur containing the cancer and replaced it with a prosthesis.


Dave survived the surgery, happy to still have a relatively well functioning leg. The doctor had said that in some cases they had to amputate the entire leg. Immediately after the surgery, Dave was walking around on crutches. He would be able to lose them after he healed completely, should regain full function of his knee, but would probably never play football again. It looked like his promising football career would die in it's crib, before it could even walk. At least he still had Ashley. That is, until she broke up with him. Some people might think it heartless, but I didn't really blame her. You see these people on talk shows all the time, people who cheat when their significant other is sick. My guess is that they just couldn't deal with a partner who was sick, and felt trapped, but were too afraid to break it off. Nobody gets into a relationship expecting to care for, and support someone with a terminal illness, it's an exhausting proposition. Maybe they cheat actually hoping their partner will find out, creating an excuse to break it off. Maybe it's just to ease their own emotional pain. At least Ashley didn't cheat on Dave. She just couldn't handle the burden. After that, something broke inside him. Never again would he be the happy, cheerful Dave I had known. I heard him in his room, after she told him, screaming and pounding the walls with his fists for hours. He called her horrible names. Later when he finally came out, I saw his knuckles were bruised and torn.


Our family had been planning another trip to Grandpa Whelan's farm that summer, shortly after Dave's surgery. However, after Dave's diagnosis, and his crushing break-up, our parents were not sure it was the best time to make such a trip. But Dave still wanted to, he enjoyed visiting the farm, and thought the solitude might give him a chance to get his head right. So that summer, our family once again traveled to Elk City. Dave would hobble around the house on what he called his “sick sticks”, while I spent most of my time outside, exploring. There were plenty of lizards, frogs, and newts to harass, along with the occasional deer, or rafter of wild turkeys spotted scurrying through the trees. I rediscovered the old well on one such outing. It had become harder to discern amongst the grass and weeds which surrounded it, as they had almost reached the height of the stone wall surrounding the well, and obscured it from view. The same thick wooden cover still lay on top, though now more weathered.


I pushed the cover aside and look into the darkness, calling, “HELLO IN THERE!”


My greeting reverberated back up at me, booming hollow and distorted, sounding like the echoing reply of another. I remembered this well … it used to grant me wishes. I had been worried a lot about my brother, at the time. Maybe he knee wouldn't heal, maybe he'd get an infection. It could leave him crippled, hobbling around with only one good leg. What if the cancer returns? That happens sometimes. A person will recover, and be healthy for a year or two, then their cancer returns with a vengeance, and their bones become rotting, black masses. Their own body turning against them. Maybe I could wish for him to get better, the well had worked before. But I felt somewhat hesitant to try, as though testing it too much might break whatever power it possessed. What if it only has a limited amount of power, and it was running out? What if it was all just a coincidence, and it never really granted anything? What if it was just a regular old well, as Dave had said? I checked my pockets for change and found a few coins. Of the various denominations I held in my hands, I selected a quarter. If this wish was going to work, it would take more than a penny. I sent my quarter spiraling towards it final resting place below the water, and wish for my brother to get better.


In the following months, Dave recuperated. It was a painstaking process, but with time, and physical therapy, his leg healed, and he regained full use of it. In following doctor visits he was pronounced fit as a fiddle, the doctors own words. While I was overjoyed by my brothers recovery, I was at the same time worried by a troubling turn of events. As my brother's health continued to improve, so did Grandpa Whelan's health begin to decline. He wasted away to nothing, almost over night, then one day a blood clot lodged in his brain, and he stroked out. I felt some concern that I might be at least partially responsible. Strange thoughts begin to nag me, picking at the back of my brain. What if that well was a monkey's paw, and grandpa's death was the dead-son-at-the-door I paid for my brothers health?


When I was born, I was cursed with an inquisitive mind. I say cursed, for while there are some benefits, there are significant disadvantages, as well. You always think of all the things that could go wrong, and all the wrong things you could have done. My Grandfather's death weighed on my shoulders like a cross, and I dragged his ghost around like a ball and chain shackled to my leg. Drinking helped a little, drugs helped more. After some experimenting, I found a combination of the two, that helped very much. Apparently some people don't like being around someone who is always whacked out of their mind, because my family seemed to want to spend less time around me. At 19 my parents politely asked me to leave their house. I moved in with a friend who was a little more understanding of my proclivities, and even shared an interest in some of them himself.


Dave could no longer play football, but still went to college, and majored in agricultural science. He had always been interested in farming, had even been a member of Future Farmers of America. Since Grandpa's death he had shown in interest in taking over the farm. Dave was always the confident one. He seemed to succeed in everything he did. He earned his degree, then moved out to Idaho and into grandpa's old place. The next ten years of my life were spent wandering in and out of a haze, like that first drive down highway 14. When I emerged from the fog, I was rapidly approaching 30 with no friends or accomplishments to speak of. I searched for help in clawing my way out of this hole I had dug myself into, but my parents no longer wanted to associate with me. It was my bother, who I had somehow resented for so many years, who took me in.


“Come stay at the farm for as long as you want”, he told me, “I'd be glad to have you. Besides, it gets kind of lonely out here by myself”.


And so once again, I found myself traveling towards Elk City, Idaho, but this time it was just me in my old Subaru. Things seemed smaller than I remembered – or maybe I was just bigger. Maybe I remembered things larger than they were, like they had grown in my mind, over the years. I stopped at a gas station in Grangeville, to fill up and use the restroom. The walls inside the bathroom stalls were covered in graffiti, mostly illegible gang tags. When I was younger, bathroom graffiti was of a higher quality. Often you would find a funny message, or dirty limerick, sometimes a number to call for a good time. Whatever it was, it was more entertaining to look at than contemporary graffiti. As I turned to leave the stall, I saw someone had scrawled on the inside of the door in big block letters, “I AM WATCHING YOU”. I couldn't help but look over my shoulder, as I washed my hands.


I stopped in the gas station's small market to buy a candy bar and a can of iced tea. When I opened the door to the cooler and grabbed my iced tea, it was barely cooler than room temperature. The clerk charged me $1.29 for iced tea with 99¢ printed on the can, but I was tired from driving all day, so I didn't argue. On my way out, I noticed a bulletin board layered with missing persons fliers, the ones on bottom yellowed from age.


“Jessica Gilchrist – Age: 19 – Height: 5' 6'' – Hair: Blonde – Eyes: Green – Last Seen Wearing A Blue And White Checked Dress – Any Info. Please Call 208-555-7461” read one of the fliers, below a black and white photo of a pretty young girl.


So many people disappear, like they just blipped out of existence, and are never heard from again. It happens so often that we accept it as the norm. Sometimes I wish I could disappear.

It was late by the time I arrived at the farm. As I drove down the dirt road to the house, I thought I saw a pair of eyes in the woods, reflecting my headlights. I pulled up in front of the house, and saw the porch light flick on, Dave must have been waiting up for me. I climbed out of the vehicle, and saw him standing at the door to greet me. We hugged and patted each other on the back with our fists, as men do.


“It's good to see you”, said Dave.


“You too”, I smiled at him, “You're looking good. I guess farm life is treating you well”.


“Eh, can't complain,” Dave nodded, “Come on in, it's cold as a witches tit out here”. His breath billowed out in clouds as he spoke.


I kicked my shoes on the welcome mat, and stepped inside. The house looked pretty much as I remembered it, perhaps a bit smaller.


“Well, it's past my bed time, I'm going to head up to bed”, Dave called, as he ascended the stairs, “Your old room is made up for you. Make yourself at home, I'll see you tomorrow”.


I dropped my bags at the foot of the stairway, and headed for the kitchen. I intended to make myself at home, as Dave had suggested, and if I were at home right now, I would definitely fix myself a drink. I searched the kitchen cabinets, and found the liquor in one, on a shelf above the coffee and tea. Picking out a decent bottle of bourbon, I selected a tumbler from the cabinet I had seen the glasses in a moment ago, and filled it with about four fingers worth of honey colored liquid. I left the bottle out on the counter, in case I decided to come back for seconds, and wandered into the living room while sipping my drink. One of the first things I noticed, was that Dave had no television in his living room. The TV was usually what a person designed their entire room around. Find the best spot for it, then you put the couch across the room facing the TV, coffee table in front of the couch, etc … What did he do with all his time up here? Maybe he had a TV up in his room. I particularly enjoy television when isolated, it gives me a feeling of connection with the rest of the world. Hell, maybe he liked to read. The second thing I noticed, was the old side-by-side double-barreled shotgun mounted above the fireplace.


Turning, I gazed out the front bay window, and I pondered life's essential questions, but could no longer see the glowing pair of eyes which had appeared to greet me. It seemed safe, so I stepped out onto the porch for a smoke. The tip of my cigarette glowed like a dying sun as I inhaled deeply of the burning leaf. The first hit always tasted the best. I felt like an Indian out there under that ocean of sky, with the tobacco smoke in my lungs, and the earth below me. I felt like I should be dancing with wolves. But the Indians had gone the way of the buffalo, and I was only a sad little white boy. It was quiet out here, except for the occasional cricket chirp. It felt uncomfortable, like the quiet was somehow menacing. Hungrily gobbling up any noise which dare disturb its silence, lurking out there in the dark, waiting to gobble me up without a sound. Turning my head to the side as I blew a cloud of smoke, I noticed a faint line of pulsing light out towards the tree-line. I stood sipping my bourbon, and smoking my cigarette, and thinking for some reason I should know what that was.


I awoke the next morning feeling like the desiccated husk of a long-dead bug, laying with its feet curled, in a forgotten corner. It got worse when I tried to stand up. I swayed back and forth like a roadside air dancer flailing his idiot arms in the air; bending forward, near collapse, before re-inflating, and snapping back up, to advertise great deals inside. The headache hit the front of my brain with the incandescent pop of a flash bulb. I gingerly took my first few steps, testing my balance. It took a moment, but once I had my sea legs, I found that I could hold myself upright fairly well. Walking across the room brought the nausea roiling in my stomach, and creeping up my throat. I began to salivate salty, swallowing to keep my gorge down, and managed to avoid purging my stomach. Hobbling downstairs I made my way into the kitchen for some coffee. I wouldn't be able to stomach any food for at least a few hours. Opening the cabinet, I saw the half-empty bottle of bourbon I had been working on last night, returned to its shelf. I could not recall placing the bottle back in the cabinet. Now that I thought about it, I could not remember bringing up my luggage either, yet it had been there in my my room, this morning. But then again, as I cast my mind back, I did not seem to remember much of anything past a certain point last night. Having experienced that uncomfortable lack of memory before, I was grateful for being isolated out here, where I couldn't get into any serious blackout trouble.


As I started the coffee brewing, I realized I had not seen, nor heard Dave this morning, and wondered where he was. Looking out the front window I saw his truck still parked outside, so I figured he must be around the farm somewhere. It looked like there was a light on in the barn, he was probably milking the cows, or whatever it is that farmers do. I went to pour myself some coffee, and when I pulled the pot out, a few drops fell onto the hotplate below where they popped and sizzled. With coffee in hand, I went outside to smoke my first cigarette of the day. As the caffeine and nicotine flooded my system, the headache eased a little, and I wandered around to stretch my aching muscles. When I was younger, hangovers were never this bad. They say after the age of 25, your body starts dying, and I believe it. Things don't seem to work quite as well as they once did. Sometimes I wake up in the morning feeling like an old man, my joints popping and creaking.


Lost in my own thoughts, I had wandered towards the old well, and now stood in front of it. The plants and grass which had once grown around it were now withered and brown, with the carcasses of dead insects littering the ground amongst them. There were paw prints surrounding it, which looked too large to be a dogs. The wooden lid which covered it was now black and rotten, and on it grew colonies of strange fungus. With a grunt I lifted the lid and slid it off to one side, where it finally gave up the ghost, and collapsed into a pile of decaying matter. A loathsome, feculent odor arose from within the darkened portal, sending my stomach roiling, and causing me to grimace as I jerked my head back. Didn't I used to make wishes here? The memory seemed cloudy, like a dream, or movie, misremembered as something I had actually experienced. I could feel the edges of it in my mind, but quickly changed my train of thought, as I knew they were bad memories. I flicked my cigarette butt inside, as I turned to walk back towards the house, and heard it squelch out as it hit the water below. That putrid water must be choked with algae and little spiraling protozoa. A microcosm of life supported by decay.


When I got back to the house, Dave's mud-caked boots were on the front porch, and I found him in the kitchen, washing his hands.


“There he is! Haven't seen you all day, what have you been up to?” I asked him.


“Some of us have real work to do. Milking the cows, collecting eggs from the chicken coop, you know, farm stuff”, he said in a curt tone.


“Relax Bro, I'm just messin with ya,” I told him, holding my palms up in the air in front of me, as though to placate him.


Remembering the tracks I had seen around the well, I asked, “Hey, there aren't any wolves around here, are there?”


“There weren't any when we were kids”, he told me, “But they reintroduced them in '95. A lot of farmers don't like it, but I haven't had much trouble with them. The corpse lay still as I approached, and I saw a viscous black and green fluid oozing from the wound where half it's face used to be. It looked pretty dead, but to be sure, using the axe, I hacked off what remained of it's head.


I didn't want to leave the thing lying out there, so I resolved to bury it again. Grabbing the legs, I dragged the body behind me, and kicking the head forward every few steps, moved towards the location near the burnt-down barn where I had previously buried the body. The shovel I had used still thrust in the dirt where I had left it, Dave's only grave marker. The dirt covering the grave was undisturbed, apparently the well had somehow transported Dave's body into it's depths, and there revived him. The dirt was not as tightly packed as the first time I had dug the grave, and the soil was still damp, which made the digging easier. As I approached the depth at which I had buried him previously, my shovel struck something which did not feel like earth. I got down on my knees and cleared the dirt around it, where I saw Dave's rotting face. I screamed like a girl and scrambled out of the hole with a quickness, where I stood over it, panting from exertion, and covered with dirt. I looked first at the headless body which lay in the grass, then shifting my gaze to the body which lay half-exposed in the hole. A nervous chuckle escaped my lips. I felt that familiar fear rising inside me, vibrating and bouncing around incoherently. Somehow, the idea of the well reproducing Dave's body, was even more terrifying than the notion that it had transported him. I rolled the headless body into the pit, on top of the other dead Dave, and kicked the head in after it. I then hastily buried them together, not wanting to stay out here any longer than necessary. I couldn't be sure, but it looked like there were more eyes watching me from the woods, than when I first came outside. Like they were congregating for something.


I was at least grateful for the fact that Dave had left the liquor cabinet well stocked. I spent my days drinking until I blacked out, and my nights killing Dave. Every night a new one would climb out of the well, and after the first one, I just started throwing the bodies back in. I do not know now, how many times I have killed my brother. I lost count a while ago, and eventually gave up when my supply of shotgun shells began running low. I suppose I could calculate, by counting the remaining shells, how many time's I've killed him, but it doesn't matter. Killing him every night made no difference, in the end. Now I lay here and listen to him outside, taunting and cajoling me. I know now what I must do. Tomorrow, after the sun rises, I will go out to the old well, and before throwing myself inside, will making one last wish. I would tell you what it is, but then it wouldn't come true.


After numerous reports of suspicious activity, and noises emanating from the Whelan Farm, authorities were dispatched to the location. The journal of Thomas Whelan, which contains the preceding account of events, was found in his room. The old well was drained and searched, but all they found were a handful of coins. The property was canvassed with corpse-sniffing dogs, and the remains of over 300 individuals were discovered, dating back to the early 1800's. Unearthed where Thomas Whelan claimed to have buried the bodies of his brother, were the bodies of Dave Whelan and Thomas Whelan, himself.
